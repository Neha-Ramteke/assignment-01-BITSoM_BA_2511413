## Storage Systems

I would use a combination of storage systems because the hospital has different data needs across its four goals. For predicting patient readmission risk, I would use a **data lake** to store large volumes of historical treatment data, doctor notes, discharge summaries, and ICU streams in raw or semi-structured form. This is useful for machine learning because it supports flexible feature extraction and model training. For doctors querying patient history in plain English, I would use a **vector database** built on top of embeddings generated from clinical notes, discharge summaries, and patient history text. This enables semantic search instead of exact keyword matching. For monthly management reporting such as bed occupancy and department-wise costs, I would use a **data warehouse**, because it is optimized for structured analytical queries, aggregation, and dashboard reporting. For live operational activities such as patient registration, admissions, discharge updates, and billing transactions, I would use an **OLTP relational database** such as PostgreSQL or MySQL. For real-time ICU device data, I would first ingest the stream through a streaming layer and store it in the **data lake**, where it can later feed both analytics and AI models.

## OLTP vs OLAP Boundary

The transactional system ends at the **OLTP relational database**, where live patient operations are recorded and updated in real time. This includes current patient records, appointments, treatment events, and billing transactions. The analytical system begins when data is extracted from OLTP and moved through ETL or streaming pipelines into the **data warehouse**, **data lake**, and **vector database**. The data warehouse supports reporting and trend analysis, while the data lake supports machine learning and long-term historical analysis. The vector database supports semantic retrieval over text-heavy medical records.

## Trade-offs

One major trade-off in this design is **architectural complexity**. Using multiple storage systems improves performance and fit-for-purpose design, but it also increases integration effort, governance overhead, and maintenance complexity. To mitigate this, I would implement strong data governance, clear ownership of each layer, and automated pipelines for synchronization and validation. I would also define a clear data contract between OLTP, lake, warehouse, and vector systems so that consistency and data freshness can be monitored systematically.
