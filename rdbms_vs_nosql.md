## Database Recommendation

For a healthcare startup building a patient management system, I would recommend **MySQL** as the primary database. Healthcare data is highly sensitive and operationally critical. Patient records, prescriptions, appointments, billing, and treatment histories require strong consistency, reliability, and transactional integrity. MySQL follows the **ACID** model, which ensures atomicity, consistency, isolation, and durability. These properties are important in healthcare because an incomplete or inconsistent transaction can create serious clinical and legal risks. For example, if a prescription update succeeds but the billing or patient history update fails, the system may become unsafe.

MongoDB, on the other hand, is often associated with **BASE**-oriented behavior and flexible schemas. It is useful when data structures vary significantly or when high scalability and rapid iteration are priorities. However, for the core patient management system, consistency is more important than schema flexibility. Under the **CAP theorem**, in distributed systems there is often a trade-off between consistency and availability during partition events. For healthcare operations, consistency should be prioritized over eventual consistency.

My recommendation would partially change if the startup also needed a **fraud detection module**. Fraud detection often involves semi-structured data, rapidly changing features, logs, device fingerprints, and behavioral signals. That workload may benefit from MongoDB because of its flexible schema and ability to handle evolving data models. Therefore, the best architecture may be hybrid: use **MySQL for core patient and transactional records**, and use **MongoDB or another NoSQL system for fraud analytics, event data, and flexible investigative workflows**. This gives strong integrity where it is essential and flexibility where it is strategically useful.
