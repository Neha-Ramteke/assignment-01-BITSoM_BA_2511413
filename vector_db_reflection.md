## Vector DB Use Case

A traditional keyword-based database search would not be sufficient on its own for a law firm searching large 500-page contracts through natural language questions. Keyword search works well when the user knows the exact terms present in the document, but legal language is often complex, indirect, and highly varied. For example, a lawyer may ask, “What are the termination clauses?” while the contract may use phrases such as “grounds for early exit,” “agreement may be discontinued,” or “rights of either party to terminate.” A pure keyword search may miss relevant sections if the exact words do not match.

A vector database solves this problem by enabling **semantic search**. In such a system, the contract would first be broken into smaller chunks such as clauses or paragraphs. Each chunk would then be converted into an embedding, which is a numerical vector representing its meaning. These embeddings would be stored in a vector database. When a lawyer asks a question in plain English, the question is also converted into an embedding, and the system retrieves the most semantically similar chunks rather than only exact keyword matches.

This approach is especially useful in legal search because it improves recall and helps surface relevant passages even when wording differs. Therefore, the best system would combine both methods: keyword search for exact terms and vector search for semantic understanding. The vector database would serve as the core retrieval layer for finding meaningfully related contract sections quickly and accurately.
